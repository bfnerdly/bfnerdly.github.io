<section class="journal-shell journal-section">
  <div class="journal-section__header">
    <h2 class="journal-section__title">Current Research</h2>
    <span class="journal-section__meta">Harvard University</span>
  </div>

  <figure class="journal-figure journal-block-space">
    <img src="/assets/images/zombies1.jpg" alt="Zombie flies used in current research">
    <figcaption>A group of infected flies. Image by Dr. Carolyn Elya.</figcaption>
  </figure>

  <div class="journal-prose">
    <p>
      In the Elya Lab, I study how parasitic infections manipulate animal behavior through neural pathways. I am especially interested in how simple nervous systems, such as those in insects, produce complex and highly stereotyped behaviors.
    </p>
    <p>
      The <em>Drosophila</em> and <em>Entomophthora muscae</em> "zombie fly" system offers a powerful model: the fungus infects and consumes the fly from within, ultimately controlling behavior through the nervous system before death.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="journal-columns journal-stack-top">
    <article class="journal-card journal-panel">
      <h3>Dysregulation of circadian behavior</h3>
      <p>
        <em>E. muscae</em> triggers dramatic behavioral changes in <em>Drosophila</em> just before dusk, including summiting, proboscis extension and adhesion, and wing raising.
      </p>
      <p>
        Our lab has found that infected flies also lose their characteristic morning activity peak on the final day of life. I am investigating which neural changes drive that circadian impairment.
      </p>
    </article>

    <article class="journal-card journal-panel">
      <h3>Neural basis of summiting</h3>
      <p>
        Prior work in our lab identified the pars intercerebralis to corpus allatum circuit as essential to summiting, implicating juvenile hormone release in the process.
      </p>
      <p>
        I am now exploring how activity in this circuit changes to enable precise, parasite-driven behavioral control.
      </p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="journal-shell journal-section">
  <div class="journal-section__header">
    <h2 class="journal-section__title">Graduate Research</h2>
    <span class="journal-section__meta">Emory University</span>
  </div>

  <figure class="journal-figure journal-block-space">
    <img src="/assets/images/smice.jpg" alt="Spiny mice used in graduate research">
    <figcaption>A group of spiny mice. Image by Dr. Aubrey Kelly.</figcaption>
  </figure>

  <div class="journal-prose">
    <p>
      During my PhD in the Kelly Lab, my research focused on the neural mechanisms of social behavior across non-model rodent species. I began by investigating how pair bonding affects nonapeptide-producing neurons in prairie voles and later found related effects in Mongolian gerbils.
    </p>
    <p>
      I then studied the communally breeding spiny mouse, <em>Acomys dimidiatus</em>, to explore the neural circuits that guide group affiliation. This work showed that males prefer larger peer groups and use kinship cues in social decisions.
    </p>
    <p>
      Through chemogenetic manipulations, I identified an anterior cingulate cortex to lateral septum projection that regulates group size preference. I also helped develop and validate experimental tools for spiny mice, including iDISCO, fiber photometry, CRISPR-Cas9, Cre-dependent chemogenetics, and neural tracing approaches.
    </p>
  </div>
</section>
